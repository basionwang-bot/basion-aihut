## 📖 What You'll Learn

By the end of this lesson, you'll be able to deliver a complete **e-commerce product page content package** — distilled selling points, a structured product description, and platform-specific adaptations — all bundled and ready to hand off.

First, let's talk about the life-or-death logic of product pages.

When a shopper lands on your listing, the question in their head isn't "Is this good?" — it's "Can I trust that this product will actually help me?" That trust gets built (or lost) in a few seconds of scrolling.

So a product page is fundamentally **a persuasion funnel**: from "What am I looking at?" to "Why do I need this?" to "I'm buying it right now." Every screen has a job. There are no wasted screens.

You need to understand the distinct "physical laws" of each platform:

**Amazon / Shopify product page**: Shoppers are in research mode — sitting at home, comparing options, willing to read. Listings can be detailed and thorough. Trust signals matter: brand storefronts, review counts, comparison tables, detailed specs. Buyers often read for a while before deciding.

**Etsy / budget marketplaces (eBay)**: Shoppers are drawn to uniqueness, handmade feel, and story. The copy should be warm and personal — who made it, why, what makes it special. Price and social proof still matter, but the emotional connection to the maker or the craft is often the real closer.

**TikTok Shop / Instagram Shopping**: Shoppers are scrolling video when something stops them — the product page needs to match that energy. Visual impact first, minimal text, short punchy sentences. The first screen must lock attention instantly. This is impulse territory.

## 🧠 Core Principles (Internalize These)

**1. Lead with pain, then introduce the solution**

The most common beginner mistake: opening with "This product uses XX material and features XX technology."

The reader's response: So what? What does that have to do with me?

The right sequence: first make the buyer feel the ache of not having this — "Scrambling for your keys every morning before work…" or "Third all-nighter this week, and your face shows it…" — then your product walks in like a rescue.

Poke the pain first. Then offer the relief.

**2. A selling point is not a feature — it's the benefit the feature delivers**

Feature: "Active Noise Cancellation technology"
Selling point: "Tune out the open office and stay in flow — no more getting pulled out of deep work by background chatter"

Quick test: after every feature, ask "So what? What does this actually mean for the buyer?" — if you can answer that clearly, you have a selling point.

**3. Every screen has a job — no wasted screens**

Picture a buyer scrolling from top to bottom. Each screen solves one problem:
- Screen 1: Hook attention (What am I looking at? Is this worth my time?)
- Screen 2: Build desire (Why do I need this in my life?)
- Screen 3+: Eliminate doubt (Is it quality? Is it worth the price? How does it compare?)
- Final screen: Drive action (Buy now / Add to cart)

**4. Each platform speaks a different language**

Amazon: Thorough, credible, comparison-friendly — detailed is fine
Etsy: Personal, warm, story-driven — make it feel human and handcrafted
TikTok Shop: High-energy, emotional, visual-first — short sentences, big impact

**5. Drafts are drafts. Publishing is the owner's job.**

You write the copy. You hand it over. That's where your work ends — no logging in, no submitting, no publishing.

## 🛠 How to Do It

### Step 1: Selling Point Extraction Worksheet

Before writing a single word of copy, work through this worksheet with the owner:

```
[PRODUCT BASICS]
Category: _____
Price point: _____
Target buyer: Who buys this? (age / lifestyle / use case)

[PAIN POINT EXCAVATION]
What's the buyer's biggest frustration right now?
How are they getting by without this product? (What's wrong with their current workaround?)

[CORE SELLING POINTS — max 5, ranked by priority]
1. _____ → What this means for the buyer: _____
2. _____ → What this means for the buyer: _____
3. _____ → What this means for the buyer: _____
(optional) 4. _____
(optional) 5. _____

[COMPETITIVE EDGE]
Compared to similar products, where does this one win? What's different?
(If there's no clear edge, say so honestly — don't invent one)

[REAL CUSTOMER FEEDBACK] (if available)
What do buyers most often praise?
What questions do buyers ask most? (These should be pre-answered in the listing)
```

### Step 2: Product Page Structure (Standard + Platform Variations)

**Standard Structure (Amazon — full version)**

```
[Hero image area] Main visual + one-line core selling point (15 words or fewer)

Screen 1: Scene hook
  → A real-life scenario or pain point your target buyer recognizes
  → Goal: make the buyer think "That's exactly me"

Screen 2: Product reveal + key selling points
  → Product images (multiple angles)
  → 3–5 core selling points, one sentence each + a close-up image per point

Screen 3: Features / materials in detail
  → Each feature detail with image + text explanation
  → Format: "[Feature name] → What this means for you: ..."

Screen 4: Comparison (optional but strongly recommended)
  → Before/after · with/without · vs. competitors
  → Let buyers see the difference, not just read about it

Screen 5: Real customer reviews (if available)
  → Pick the most persuasive ones, pair with images

Screen 6: How-to / FAQ
  → Pre-answer the 3–5 questions buyers ask most
  → Reduces returns and sets expectations

Screen 7: Close + call to action
  → Brand / store trust signal
  → "Order today…" / "Limited time…"
```

**Etsy / eBay Simplified Version**

```
Skip the scene-setting — open with warmth and story instead:
Screen 1: Hero image + one sentence on what makes this special (handmade, unique, story)
Screen 2: Product images (multiple angles) + key specs or materials
Screen 3: The maker's story or the "why behind the product" — keep it genuine
Screen 4: Real buyer reviews + shipping / return policy
```

**TikTok Shop / Instagram Shopping Version**

```
Screen 1: Big visual impact (bold image / strong color contrast / before-after)
Minimize text — let the visuals do the heavy lifting
Write in short punchy fragments throughout: "This one's different." "You'll get it when you try it." "No fluff, just results."
```

### Copy Style Reference

| Platform | Recommended Style | Example |
|----------|------------------|---------|
| Amazon | Detailed, trustworthy, thorough | "Crafted from premium XX-grade materials through a 48-step process… every detail designed so you can buy with confidence" |
| Etsy / eBay | Direct, conversational, value-focused | "Same factory construction as the $60 version — we're just not paying for the brand name. You're welcome." |
| TikTok Shop | Emotional, scene-driven, punchy | "I didn't believe it either until I actually used it…" |

### Long Copy vs. Short Copy

```
Product price < $15:    Short listing (3–4 screens is enough — buyers don't want to read much)
Product price $15–$75:  Standard listing (5–7 screens — you need to build enough trust)
Product price > $75:    Detailed listing (8+ screens — remove every last doubt before they decide)
```

- A handmade craft or artisan food item (Etsy)
- An online course or learning product (Amazon / TikTok Shop)

**What you need to deliver:**

1. **Selling point extraction worksheet**: Fill out the full worksheet above — product basics, pain point excavation, 5 selling points with feature-to-benefit conversions, and competitive edge.

2. **Full Amazon listing copy**: Write a 7-screen copy script following the standard structure. For each screen, specify: the screen's job + the copy text + image direction (you don't need to make actual images — just write "this screen uses a photo of XX showing YY").

3. **Etsy adaptation**: Starting from the Amazon version, simplify into a 4-screen Etsy-style listing. Note what you changed and why.

4. **TikTok Shop adaptation**: Write the Screen 1 hero copy (20 words or fewer) + full short-form text for the entire listing (each line 15 words or fewer).

5. **Self-check**: Review against these two criteria — (1) Does every selling point have a clear "what this means for the buyer" explanation? (2) Is the tone noticeably different across the three platform versions?

6. **Skill card**: Distill the e-commerce product page workflow into `skills/ecommerce-detail.md`.
