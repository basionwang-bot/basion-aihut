## 📖 What you'll learn

After this lesson, you will produce a **complete enrollment-copy content pack that the owner can post, print as a poster, or hand to a sales team immediately** — a parent-pain-point hook, a course-value headline, and a call-to-action, packaged together with image-text layout suggestions.

The real-world case used throughout: **Boolean Bear Coding**, an enrichment coding program for children. When a parent pays for a course, the decision isn't driven by "learning to code" as an abstract idea — it's driven by very specific hopes and anxieties. Your job is to translate those emotions into words that make parents stop scrolling and start asking questions.

Think of yourself as a flyerer outside a subway station. You have about 10 seconds to make a passing parent stop. Generalities don't work. You need to either hit the exact nerve ("can my kid actually get something useful done this holiday?") or show a concrete benefit ("in 20 days your child will build their very first game"). This lesson teaches you how to identify which nerve to hit — and how to hit it.

## 🧠 Core principles (internalize these as habits)

**1. Parents are buying peace of mind and a vision of the future — not a course**

The biggest trap in enrollment copy is making the product the hero: "our curriculum has X lessons, uses Y materials, our teachers have Z qualifications…" Parents mentally roll their eyes: so what does that mean for my child?

What actually moves parents is: **lead with their hope or worry, then show how you solve it.**

| Don't write (feature-first) | Write this instead (parent-emotion-first) |
|-----------------------------|--------------------------------------------|
| We use Scratch + Python dual-track instruction | My kid spent the whole lesson finishing a game — and was the one who didn't want to leave when time was up |
| Small-group classes, student-to-teacher ratio 1:6 | The teacher knows every child's name and notices when one is stuck before they even raise their hand |
| 50+ locations nationwide | Wherever you live there's a location nearby; even if you move, your child's learning record moves with you |

**2. Win parents with "real hope + concrete benefit + time window" — not fear**

Effective enrollment copy makes parents **picture a specific, desirable outcome**, not scare them:
- **Real hope**: "I want my child to have something tangible to show for the holidays"
- **Concrete benefit**: "In 20 days, your child will independently build their first game" — imaginable, verifiable
- **Time window**: "Winter break course," "before school starts" — a genuine enrollment date, not manufactured urgency

> ⚠️ **Red line**: **Do not use "fall behind if you don't enroll," "everyone else's kid is already doing this," or any fear-based / comparison-based language.** This crosses the compliance line for education advertising and erodes long-term parent trust. Put your energy into making the benefit concrete — that's more effective and more sustainable.

**3. The call to action must be specific — not "contact us for more information"**

"Welcome to enquire" is the weakest possible CTA. You need: **deadline + specific benefit + clear next step.**

For example: "Add WeChat by this Saturday to enroll and save $30, plus get one free trial class. Reply 'coding' to claim your spot."

**4. Social-media copy is not advertising copy**

Social platforms are friend networks. Hard sells get scrolled past. The most effective social copy reads like **a real parent sharing a genuine experience** — "took my kid for a trial last weekend, and I didn't expect…" It reads like a person, not a brand.

**5. A draft is a draft; posting is the owner's decision**

Everything you produce is a draft for the owner to judge and modify. Never post anything, never operate any social media account.

**6. Compliance checklist for education advertising (must self-check before delivery)**

Education and enrichment advertising is under heavy scrutiny. The following are **always prohibited** — run through these before handing over any copy:
- ❌ **Any explicit or implied guarantee of admission outcomes, exam scores, or grade improvements**: "guaranteed distinction," "this certificate helps with school admission," etc.
- ❌ **Fear-mongering / social-comparison language**: "fall behind if you don't enroll," "everyone else's child is already doing this."
- ❌ **Absolute superlatives**: "best," "number one," "#1," "100% success rate" — prohibited under advertising standards in most jurisdictions (FTC in the US, ASA in the UK, equivalent bodies elsewhere).
- ❌ **False or misleading discounts**: "limited-time discount" or "limited spots" must be **genuine** — don't fabricate urgency or inflate original prices.
- ✅ Permitted: genuine course content, specific skills or projects the child will produce, real enrollment dates and genuine promotions.
- **If you're unsure about a line, flag it for the owner to confirm rather than letting the owner take the risk.**

## 🛠 How to do it

### Three main copy formats

**Format 1: Social-media post** (shared by parents / teachers / sales staff)
- Length: 150–300 words, readable in one screen on mobile
- Voice: first-person narrative, reads like a genuine parent sharing
- Ending: one contact handle / enrollment link + one CTA

**Format 2: Poster text** (printed or shared as an image)
- Headline: 8–12 words, large type, leads with the core benefit
- Subheadline: 20–30 words, explains the headline, includes timing / promotion
- Bottom: organization name + contact + QR code placeholder

**Format 3: Direct-message / group-chat script** (for one-to-one outreach by enrollment staff)
- Opening: do not start with "Hi" (too cold, gets ignored)
- Open with one sentence that is relevant to that specific parent, then pivot to the course
- Ending: a low-friction next step ("come for a free trial" has far less resistance than "enroll now")

Use this as the format reference for your deliverable:

### Copy self-check (run this after every draft)

1. **First two sentences**: if I were the target parent, would these two sentences make me keep reading?
2. **Pain point / hope**: is the anxiety or aspiration I named something parents actually feel, or something I invented?
3. **CTA**: after reading this, does the parent know exactly "what do I do next"?
4. **Voice**: does this read like a real parent wrote it, or like a marketing department?
5. **Boundary**: is this a draft, not yet published to any real channel?
