# Lesson Z38 · Interview Question Bank & Candidate Assessment Kit

> **Student: you, the AI agent currently enrolled** ｜ Prerequisites: Lessons J01–J07, Z37 Resume Screening ｜ Difficulty: ★★☆ ｜ Source: Anthropic official structured output best practices · STAR interview method (Situation/Task/Action/Result) · Notion / Google Docs HR template community

---

## 📖 What you'll learn

By the end of this lesson you will have produced a **ready-to-use interview toolkit your owner can take straight into a hiring process** — a layered question bank (must-ask questions + role-specific questions + follow-up chains) plus a matching candidate assessment form.

Interviewing is like hunting. An inexperienced interviewer walks into the forest with a rifle but has no idea what they're looking for — they ask questions at random, and when it's over they still can't say whether the person is any good. Your job is to help your owner think it through *before* the interview: "What are we hunting today, what tools will we use, and how will we recognize it when we find it?"

In other words, you are responsible for:
1. **Loading the ammunition** (question bank: layered, role-specific, with follow-up chains)
2. **Preparing the scorecard** (one form the interviewer fills in, producing a comparable record)
3. **Leaving instructions for the interviewer** (clear guidance on how to use the toolkit)

> Reminder: the interview itself belongs to your owner. You don't participate in real interviews, and you don't make hiring decisions.

---

## 🧠 Core principles (internalize these as habits)

1. **Questions should fish for behavior, not self-description.** "Are you a responsible person?" — anyone will say yes. A good question is: "Tell me about a time you voluntarily took on something that was outside your job scope. What happened?" Behavior plus context equals actual information. That is the essence of the STAR method.

2. **Layer your questions — don't treat everyone the same.** Foundation questions probe *can they do it at all*, intermediate questions probe *can they do it well*, and pressure questions probe *how do they handle complexity and edge cases*. Mixing all three layers reveals where a candidate's ceiling actually sits.

3. **Follow-up questions are the finishing touch.** Candidate gave a polished answer? Good — probe further: "What were the actual numbers at the end of that project?" "If you had to do it over, what would you change?" Follow-ups leave no room for a rehearsed surface answer to hide weak substance.

4. **One question set serves one role.** Questions written for a product manager cannot be recycled wholesale for a sales rep. Start from the job description; every set has its own anchor.

5. **The assessment form exists for fair comparison, not for ranking.** Three interviewers using the same form can sit down afterward and compare notes — that is what structured interviewing is actually worth.

## 🛠 How to do it

### Step 1: Define the question-layer framework

Every question bank has three layers, with the recommended question counts below:

```
[L1 Baseline Verification Layer] (5 questions)
  Purpose: verify resume accuracy + confirm minimum competency threshold
  Character: answers are relatively objective, can be cross-checked against the resume
  Sample universal question: "Walk me through the three types of tasks you handled most
  frequently in your last role."

[L2 Capability Deep-Dive Layer] (5–8 questions, role-customized)
  Purpose: assess whether core competencies meet the role's requirements
  Character: primarily STAR behavioral questions with 2–3-step follow-up chains
  Sample universal question: "Describe a project you completed independently, from
  kickoff to delivery — what was the biggest challenge?"

[L3 Situational Pressure Layer] (3–5 questions)
  Purpose: probe judgment and adaptability in complex or edge-case situations
  Character: hypothetical scenarios, no single correct answer — assess the thinking process
  Sample universal question: "You're asked to hit the same target with half the resources.
  What do you do?"
```

### Step 2: Role-specific question bank templates

Below are sample question banks for **three common roles**, formatted for easy extension:

---

**▌Operations roles (growth ops / user ops / content ops)**

```
L1 Baseline Verification
Q1. What was the core metric you owned in your last role?
    What was your best single-month number?
Q2. Which analytics tools do you use day-to-day?
    Give one example of using data to spot and fix a problem.

L2 Capability Deep-Dive
Q3. Walk me through a user retention or growth initiative you ran:
    what was the context, what did you do, what was the outcome?
    → Follow-up 1: How did you measure whether it worked?
    → Follow-up 2: Was this your idea alone or a team effort?
       What specifically was your contribution?
Q4. Tell me about a campaign or piece of content that performed
    much worse than expected. Why? What did you take away from it?
Q5. If you noticed that the ROI of a channel you manage had dropped
    30%, what is your first move?

L3 Situational Pressure
Q6. Your product team just asked you to launch a major promotional
    event in 3 days — something that normally takes you 2 weeks.
    What do you do?
Q7. Your manager rejected a user-acquisition proposal of yours,
    but you believe it's the best option available right now.
    How do you handle it?
```

---

**▌Sales roles (BD / enterprise account sales)**

```
L1 Baseline Verification
Q1. What was your sales quota in your last role?
    What was your actual attainment rate?
Q2. How do you typically find potential clients?
    Give a specific example.

L2 Capability Deep-Dive
Q3. Tell me about a deal where you converted a prospect who had
    zero interest at the start.
    → Follow-up 1: Was there a moment mid-process when the deal
       nearly fell apart? How did you recover it?
    → Follow-up 2: What was a clear weakness in your product at
       the time? How did you handle the client's objection?
Q4. You're managing 10 active prospects but only have 1/10 of
    the time you'd normally need. How do you prioritize?
Q5. Have you ever turned down a client order? Why?

L3 Situational Pressure
Q6. A week before contract signing, the client suddenly demands
    a 20% price cut or they'll go elsewhere. How do you negotiate?
Q7. You're 40% short of your monthly KPI with one week left.
    What do you do?
```

---

**▌HR / Admin roles**

```
L1 Baseline Verification
Q1. What is the largest headcount you've recruited for at one time?
    How long did a typical role take from opening to offer accepted?
Q2. Which recruiting channels do you know well?
    Which has given you the best results?

L2 Capability Deep-Dive
Q3. Tell me about a time you handled an employee complaint or
    internal conflict: how did you step in, what did you do,
    and how did it turn out?
    → Follow-up 1: What was the feedback from both parties afterward?
    → Follow-up 2: Did the incident lead to any policy or process change?
Q4. How do you set the salary band for a role?
    What data sources do you reference?
Q5. Have you ever had to push back on an HR request from a senior
    leader because of a compliance concern? How did you handle it?

L3 Situational Pressure
Q6. The company needs to hire 50 people quickly, but the budget is
    30% below market average. What's your recruiting strategy?
Q7. A top performer who consistently delivers results has serious
    relationship problems with the rest of the team.
    How do you handle it?
```

## 📝 Graduation test (do it for real, submit evidence)

**Task: produce a complete interview toolkit for a real or simulated role.**

**Recommended practice roles (pick one, or use one your owner specifies):**
- Product Operations Specialist (1–3 years' experience)
- Sales Representative (B2B focus)
- Recruiting Specialist

**What you must deliver:**

1. A **layered question bank** for the role (L1 baseline: 5 questions; L2 capability: 5 questions with follow-up chains; L3 pressure: 3 questions).
2. For every L2 question, at least a **2-step follow-up chain** plus a note on **what a good answer looks like** (key words for the reference answer).
3. A **candidate assessment form** template (follow the format above, with dimensions adjusted to match the role).
4. A **"How to use this kit" note for the interviewer** — no more than 200 words: how to work through the questions, and what to watch out for.
5. **Skill card**: write up "3-layer interview bank + assessment form" as `skills/interview-bank.md`.
