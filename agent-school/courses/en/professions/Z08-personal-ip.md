## 📖 What you'll learn

After this lesson, you can produce a complete **personal IP content matrix plan** for the owner — positioning, topic library, and column system, all delivered as a package. This isn't just "write some content." It's about helping the owner think clearly about which direction to keep producing content in, long-term.

Let's talk about where the real difficulty lies.

Writing one newsletter issue or one Instagram post is a single shot. Building a personal IP is sustained warfare — you need to put out dozens or hundreds of pieces in a given field over a year, and get more recognizable as you go. The problem is that most people never figure out their "home base." They chase trends, writing whatever's hot that week, and after a year of output, readers still have no idea who they are or why they should follow this person.

Your job is to help the owner dig a moat — surfacing their experience, knowledge, and unique perspective, then structuring it into a system that can sustainably produce content, so that followers increasingly understand "what I get when I come to this person."

**The landscape for knowledge monetization (real research, for the owner's reference):**

- **Substack** ([substack.com](https://substack.com/)): The leading platform for paid newsletters and individual creators. Supports recurring subscriptions and one-off paid posts. Low barrier to entry; no company registration required.
- **Skool / Discord** ([skool.com](https://www.skool.com/) / [discord.com](https://discord.com/)): Community-first platforms ideal for building paid communities with content archives and ongoing discussion.
- **Gumroad / Patreon** ([gumroad.com](https://gumroad.com/) / [patreon.com](https://www.patreon.com/)): Digital product and membership platforms for selling templates, guides, and tiered memberships.
- **Teachable / Thinkific** ([teachable.com](https://teachable.com/) / [thinkific.com](https://www.thinkific.com/)): Formal course publishing platforms with higher production expectations; good for structured curriculum products.

> ⚠️ Research is research — **logging in to any platform, submitting applications, or publishing any paid content must all be done by the owner personally**. Your job is to produce the planning document.

## 🧠 Core principles (internalize these as habits)

**1. The one-sentence positioning test**

The best personal IP positioning can be stated in one sentence: "They're the person who specifically helps ______ solve ______ problems."

If you can't fill in those blanks cleanly, the positioning is either too broad (says everything, means nothing) or too vague (says something, means nothing). Don't start writing content until you've helped the owner fill in that sentence.

**2. A content matrix is not "post everywhere" — it's "home base plus distribution network"**

Picture a fishing net: in the center are a few large nodes (the platforms where most energy goes), with thin threads radiating outward (other platforms used for traffic). The home base is just one or two platforms; everything else is an amplifier. Help the owner put 80% of their effort into that home base — that's what makes a content matrix actually run.

**3. A topic library is stockpiling, not cooking to order**

The breaking point for most creators is "what do I write next?" — blank mind, procrastination, incomplete drafts. The fix is building a **topic library** in advance: continuously adding ideas, half-formed thoughts, and reader questions. Your job is to build the skeleton of that library and pre-fill it with at least 20 topics.

**4. Content columns are a "label system that makes people remember you"**

Posting random standalone pieces means readers won't remember you. But if content is organized into columns — "Case Study Monday," "Monthly Tool Review," "occasional deep dives" — readers form expectations; they have a reason to come back for you. Good columns share three traits: sustainable (the owner can keep producing), distinctive (only they can do it), and anticipation-building (readers look forward to the next one).

**5. Knowledge monetization is "trust converted to cash," not "charging for content"**

Why do people pay? Not for the content itself (there's too much free content). They pay because "I trust this person, and having them organize it saves me more time than finding it myself." So paid product design should start from "what are we saving them?" — time, decisions, costly mistakes.

## 🛠 How to do it

### Step 1: Take stock of the owner's existing capital

Before any planning, map out what the owner already has:

```
[Experience audit]
- What fields has the owner been working in for 3+ years?
- Are there experiences or perspectives that outsiders would find rare?
- Have they already built an audience on any platform?

[Content inventory]
- How much published content is in the repo's content/posts/?
- What are the core themes across that content?
- Which pieces have gotten the strongest response?

[Competitive differentiation]
- In the same field, what are other creators talking about?
- What does the owner have that others don't (background / angle / audience segment)?
```

### Step 2: Produce the IP positioning document

```
[One-sentence positioning]
They're the [identity label] who specifically helps [target audience] solve [core problem].
Example: "The person who helps ordinary people who want to make content
use AI to do the work of three people with one person's effort."

[Core value proposition]
What can followers consistently expect to get? (3 items, one sentence each)

[Identity triangle]
- Expertise tag: X years of experience in [field]
- Personality tag: communication style is [X]
- Differentiator: biggest difference from other creators in the same space is [X]

[Home base selection]
- Home base (full commitment, max 2): recommend [X], because [reason]
- Distribution channels (content syndication, no extra customization): [X], [X]
- Platforms to skip for now: [X] (reason: [X])
```

### Step 3: Design the column system

```
[Fixed columns (2–4)]
For each column, specify:
- Column name
- Update frequency
- What each installment produces
- Why only the owner can pull this off

Example:
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Column: Real-World AI Tool Reviews               │
│ Cadence: twice a month                           │
│ Output: 1,000-word review + screenshots +        │
│         recommendation verdict                   │
│ Differentiator: I actually use it for weeks,     │
│   not just read the documentation                │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

[Occasional content (1–2 types)]
- Topics: spontaneous reactions / commentary on industry news
- Collaborations: joint content with other creators
```

### Step 4: Build the topic library (first batch: at least 20)

Topic library categories:

```
[Category A: Evergreen topics] (stable search volume, long-lasting value)
- Common beginner mistakes series
- Tool tutorials and how-to guides
- Classic case study breakdowns

[Category B: Trending topics] (riding current traffic)
- Commentary on industry news
- Platform algorithm or policy changes explained
- First-look reviews of new tools

[Category C: Deep-dive topics] (building professional authority)
- Systematic methodologies
- Industry trend analysis
- Comparative research
```

Each topic entry should include: headline idea + core point in one sentence + best column or platform fit + difficulty estimate.

### Step 5: Knowledge monetization product design (draft)

```
[Entry-level products ($0–$29)]
- Format: paid newsletter / digital download
- Content: the owner's most practical framework, template set, or checklist
- Platform: Substack paid post / Gumroad

[Mid-tier products ($49–$199)]
- Format: cohort / course bundle
- Content: zero-to-one solution to one specific problem
- Platform: Teachable / Skool community / Discord

[High-value products ($500+)]
- Format: 1-on-1 coaching / cohort with direct access / consulting
- Content: deep service — individual or small group
- Conversion path: free content → entry product → high-ticket offer
```

> ⚠️ The above product designs are draft suggestions — **pricing, launching, and enrollment decisions must all be made and executed by the owner personally**. Your job is to give the owner a reference framework, not to finalize or act on it.
