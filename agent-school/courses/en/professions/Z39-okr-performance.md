# Lesson Z39 · OKR Planning & Performance Reviews

> **Student: you, the AI agent currently enrolled** ｜ Prerequisites: Lessons J01–J07 ｜ Difficulty: ★★☆ ｜ Source: Anthropic official best practices (structured generation) · Lattice / Notion OKR templates · *Measure What Matters* by John Doerr (widely cited)

---

## 📖 What you'll learn

By the end of this lesson you will have produced two things:

1. **An OKR draft** — objectives and key results ready for your owner or their team to drop straight into Lattice / Notion / Excel
2. **A performance-review writeup** — a structured, data-backed summary of what was accomplished over a given period

Picture this: end of year, and your colleague Alex has to write their self-review. They've been staring at a blank form for half an hour, thinking, "I know I did a lot this year, but I can't explain what." Your job is to turn that vague sense of "did a lot" into a clear scorecard — every item has numbers, outcomes, and impact, so the manager can glance at it and immediately understand what this person actually delivered.

OKR is a one-two punch of direction and measurement: first you lock in *where you're going* (Objective), then you set 3–5 quantifiable milestones that prove whether you got there (Key Results). Sounds simple — but most people write OKRs that are either too fuzzy ("improve user satisfaction") or too granular ("reply to 50 emails a day"). Neither works. This lesson trains you to nail the balance.

---

## 🧠 Core principles (internalize these as habits)

1. **O sets the direction; KR is the ruler.** An Objective should be ambitious and motivating, but it can't be a slogan. A Key Result must be a number or a concrete deliverable — something you can clearly judge as "hit" or "missed" at the end of the quarter.

2. **KRs are not a to-do list.** "Complete Project A" is a task. "Project A launches and DAU exceeds 10,000" is a KR. The difference: KRs care about outcomes, not activities.

3. **Back every performance claim with numbers.** "Handled user operations" — zero signal. "Reached 50,000 users in Q3, 12% conversion rate, 3 points above the industry average" — real signal. Quantify everything that can be quantified; for anything that can't, spell out the scope of impact.

4. **Two to four Objectives per cycle, max.** OKR is not a wish list. If your owner rattles off seven or eight directions, it's your job to help them converge on what matters most.

5. **The draft is yours; the decisions are theirs.** OKR direction and performance ratings are set by the owner and team — you only produce a ready-to-discuss draft, not a final answer.

## 🛠 How to do it

### Step 1: OKR decomposition template

```
[Quarterly / Annual OKR Draft]
Team / Individual: ____  Cycle: ____ Year Q_

O1: [Objective title — one motivating sentence]
  KR1.1: ____ [specific, quantifiable target with a number and deadline]
  KR1.2: ____
  KR1.3: ____
  Strategic rationale: Why does this direction matter? How does it tie to company goals?

O2: [Objective title]
  KR2.1: ____
  KR2.2: ____
  ...

[Setup notes]
- Source: based on owner-provided [business direction / manager OKRs / strategic priorities]
- Suggested alignment cycle: ____
- Suggested review cadence: ____ (recommended: bi-weekly 1-on-1s + written quarterly retrospective)
```

### Step 2: OKR quality checklist

After drafting, run every OKR through this checklist:

```
✅ Can each O be explained in one sentence — "here's where we're headed"?
✅ Does every KR contain a number (or a concrete deliverable)?
✅ Is each KR an outcome, not a task? (Avoid action-oriented phrasing like "complete X")
✅ Are there four or fewer Objectives in total?
✅ At the end of the quarter, can each KR be clearly judged as met or not?
✅ Does each O explicitly connect to company / manager OKRs?
❌ Any unmeasurable words like "improve," "strengthen," or "continuously optimize"? Replace them.
❌ Any activity plans masquerading as results? Rewrite them as outcome statements.
```

### Step 3: Performance review template

```markdown
# Performance Self-Review / Work Summary
Name (or role): ____  Review period: ____  Date: ____

## Top achievements (3–5)
> Ranked by importance; one sentence + supporting data per item

1. [Achievement]: During [time period], completed [specific work]; the result was [quantified outcome], compared with [benchmark].
2. ...

## OKR progress

| KR | Target | Actual | Attainment | Notes |
|----|--------|--------|------------|-------|
| KR1.1 | ... | ... | __% | Exceeded / Missed / On track + reason |
| KR1.2 | ... | ... | __% | |

## Highlights & innovations
- (What did you proactively do that wasn't in the KRs but added value to the team?)

## What fell short
- (Which KRs weren't met? Root-cause analysis + improvement plan for next cycle)

## Priorities for next cycle
1.
2.
3.

## Resources needed
(What support from your manager or the company is needed to hit next cycle's goals?)
```

### Step 4: How to draw out information from your owner

When helping your owner shape their OKRs, ask these questions one at a time — don't fire them all at once:

```
1. "What is the single most important thing for your team this quarter / year?
   What changes for the company if you pull it off?"
2. "What metric — one you personally control — most affects that outcome?"
3. "Three months from now, which three numbers would prove you did a great job?"
4. "Any constraints to flag upfront? (Resources, dependencies on other teams, etc.)"
5. "What are your manager's OKRs? How does your Objective support theirs?"
```
