## 📖 What you'll learn

After this lesson, you will deliver a **complete YouTube video content pack the owner can take straight into production** — topic proposal + video script + title and thumbnail recommendations, bundled together.

Let's talk about YouTube's "terrain." If TikTok is street busking on a subway platform, YouTube is more like **a lecture hall where the audience showed up on purpose and expects to learn something**. Viewers will give you 20–30 minutes, but they will not tolerate filler.

YouTube's community has a few distinctive traits:

- **Searchable**: unlike TikTok which is mostly algorithm-pushed, YouTube gets a huge share of its views from search. Topic selection must account for actual search demand.
- **No padding**: good YouTube videos have one thing in common — high information density. Every minute carries something real. Padded videos get called out in the comments.
- **Long videos need structure**: YouTube's longer format needs a chapter-like feel — a clear arc of "open with expectation, develop in layers, close with synthesis," like attending a complete lecture.

Your job is to help the owner produce content that **YouTube viewers walk away from feeling they learned something** — not "reading a blog post aloud into a camera."

## 🧠 Core principles (internalize these as habits)

**1. YouTube topics: search volume + unique angle**

TikTok is mostly algorithm-push. YouTube is also heavily search-driven. So topic selection must consider two things:
- Is there search demand for this topic on YouTube? (real need)
- What is my angle and how does it differ from existing videos? (differentiation)

Good YouTube topic formula: `searched topic × unique entry angle`. For example, not "introducing AI agents," but "a real day where I let an AI agent handle my entire work — including the failures."

**2. First 30 seconds: set up expectation, not small talk**

YouTube viewers will give you more time, but in the first 30 seconds they're deciding "is this creator worth subscribing to?" These 30 seconds have one job: make the viewer understand **what they'll get by staying**.

Don't say: "Hey everyone, welcome back to my channel, today we're talking about AI."
Do say: "Today I handed my entire workday to an AI agent — emails, copy, data sorting — and some results genuinely surprised me, and some worried me. I'm telling you everything."

**3. Middle section: chapters + information density**

YouTube long-form scripts need clear chapters, each with its own small climax. A solid structure looks like:

- Opening: set expectations (30 sec – 1 min)
- Body: 3–5 chapters, each with a conclusion and an example
- Chapters need transitions (no hard cuts)
- Closing: summary + call to action

**4. Comments are part of the content**

YouTube viewers comment. You can deliberately plant a few "comment triggers" in the script — a line that gets a knowing laugh, an unexpected twist, a moment that makes people want to say "THIS, exactly." This isn't being cute; it's speaking the language of the YouTube community.

**5. Draft is draft, publish is publish**

Same iron rule as Z01: your job ends at "hand the content pack to the owner." Logging in, uploading, submitting the video — those are the owner's moves, never yours.

## 🛠 How to do it

### Three common YouTube video types (pick based on owner's needs)

| Type | Best for | Length | Characteristics |
|------|----------|--------|----------------|
| **Tutorial / how-to** | Tool walkthroughs, skill building | 5–15 min | High info density, step-by-step |
| **Review / experience** | Tool comparisons, real-use stories | 8–20 min | Objective analysis + real scenarios |
| **Opinion / analysis** | Industry trends, debates | 10–30 min | Takes a stance, has evidence, goes deep |

### Writing YouTube titles

YouTube titles have specific characteristics:
- **Contain keywords**: the words viewers search for must appear in the title (e.g., "AI agent," "Claude Code")
- **Information-rich**: the viewer should understand "what this is about" at a glance
- **Can be longer**: YouTube allows longer titles than TikTok — up to 60–70 characters is fine
- **No clickbait**: YouTube audiences have low tolerance for misleading titles — they'll dislike without watching

Good title formula: `core keyword + unique angle / outcome`

Examples:
- "I used an AI agent for a full workday: 3 things exceeded expectations, 1 gave me pause"
- "Why your AI assistant keeps disappointing you — here's where the problem is"
- "Testing Claude Code: can a non-technical user actually use it?"

### Thumbnail guidelines

YouTube thumbnails are landscape (16:9). Common approaches:
- Subject / scene + large text overlay (keep text under 6–8 words)
- Dark background + bright text for visual impact
- Left side: main image; right side: title text (follows natural eye-flow reading left-to-right)

### Video script structure template

```
[Opening (0:00–0:30)]
Set expectations: what we're doing today / what you'll see
Plant suspense: "one result really surprised me"

[Chapter 1 (0:30–X:XX)]
Sub-header: clearly states what this chapter covers
Content: conclusion → example → mini-summary
Comment trigger: a line that makes people want to respond

[Chapter 2] ...

[Final chapter]
Usually "the most important / most unexpected / highest-value" content
Saved for last to keep viewers watching to the end

[Closing]
One-sentence summary
Call to action: "subscribe," "let me know your thoughts in the comments,"
"next video I'll cover…"
```

### The content pack five-piece set (assemble all five every time)

```
[Topic proposal]      Subject + entry angle + how it differs from existing content
[Title options]       3 options, each containing core keywords
[Content positioning] Video type / target length / target audience
[Complete video script]
  - Opening (include timestamp suggestions)
  - Each chapter's script (mark comment triggers)
  - Closing
[Thumbnail notes + self-check verdict]
  Thumbnail copy suggestion + info-density self-assessment
  (how many substantive content points per minute?)
```
