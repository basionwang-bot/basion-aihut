# Lesson Z19 · PR Press Release Writing

> **Student: you, the AI agent currently enrolled** ｜ Prerequisites: Lessons J01–J07 ｜ Difficulty: ★★☆ ｜ Source: AP Style press release conventions; PR Newswire editorial standards [prnewswire.com](https://www.prnewswire.com/); TechCrunch / The Verge / Wired tech media submission practices

---

## 📖 What you'll learn

After this lesson, you will help the owner produce **a press release ready to send to a media editor**, plus a **media angle analysis list** — telling the owner which outlets are likely to be interested and what angle will maximize chances of coverage.

Here's the underlying pattern of PR: the same news requires different framing for different audiences. For the *Wall Street Journal*, you emphasize "industry significance." For *TechCrunch*, you emphasize "business logic and traction." For a niche tech blog, you lead with "technical breakthrough."

Think of yourself as a **translator shuttling between the brand and the press**. Your job is to take the founder's "We launched a new product!" and turn it into the sentence that makes a journalist's eyes light up. Once the draft is polished and the envelope is sealed — sending it out is always the owner's move.

**What makes a good press release:**

- **Inverted pyramid structure:** Most important information first, more detail as you go down. Journalists don't read to the end; the first three lines determine fate.
- **Press release ≠ advertisement:** Ads say "we're great." A press release says "here's why this matters." Self-congratulatory copy goes straight to the trash.
- **Numbers and facts are the skeleton:** Every core claim needs data (user count, growth rate, funding amount, launch date…). "A breakthrough development" without figures is worthless.

> ⚠️ This lesson produces drafts only. Do not send any draft to any media account, email address, or journalist on the owner's behalf. Publishing is the owner's action.

## 🧠 Core principles (internalize these as habits)

**1. Identify the news hook before you write a word**

The most common cause of press release death: no news hook. "We upgraded our product" is not news. "We helped 500 small businesses cut labor costs by 40%" is news.

Before writing, answer this question: "Is there a single fact in this story that would make a stranger say, 'Oh really? Seriously?'" — If yes, write the release. If no, there's nothing to write.

**2. The headline is the kill shot — one sentence decides everything**

Journalists receive dozens of pitch emails a day and spend about 10 seconds on each. Your headline must accomplish two things: say clearly "who did what" + "why it matters / how big it is."

Good headline examples:
- "AI Startup Hits 1 Million Monthly Active Users — Outpacing Comparable US Competitors at Same Stage"
- "Berlin-based SaaS Company Closes Series A, Claims AI Will Replace 70% of Traditional Customer Service Roles"

**3. The lead is the heart of the release**

The lead (first paragraph) is typically 50–100 words. Cram in the 5W1H (who / what / when / where / why / how). A reader who finishes only the lead should understand the full story.

**4. Adapt tone for different outlet types**

| Outlet type | Cares about | Tone |
|-------------|------------|------|
| Wire services (AP, Reuters) | Industry significance, verifiable facts | Formal, neutral, precise |
| Tech news (TechCrunch, The Verge, Wired) | Business logic, competitive dynamics, growth | Sharp, opinionated, insight-driven |
| Niche/product media (Product Hunt, specialist blogs) | Technical depth, user value, product design | Professional, enthusiastic, specific |
| Broader business press (Forbes, Fast Company) | Market size, founder narrative, societal impact | Narrative-friendly, accessible |

**5. The boilerplate cannot be skipped**

Every press release ends with a standardized company description — fixed format, fixed content, unchanged from release to release. It lets editors quickly understand the company's context and is also where brand identity compounds over time.

## 🛠 How to do it

### Standard press release structure

```
[HEADLINE]
Format: Company/product name + core action + scale/impact/highlight
Length: 10–20 words; no more than 25

[LEAD]
One paragraph, 50–100 words
Covers: who + did what + when + why it matters / how significant
Don't open with "Recently..." or vague scene-setting — state the facts directly

[BODY — Paragraph 1: The story core]
Expand on "what this is"
Include the key data points / facts
150–200 words

[BODY — Paragraph 2: Context and significance]
Why now? What problem does this solve?
Industry data or trends as backdrop
150–200 words

[BODY — Paragraph 3: Differentiators]
How is this different from existing solutions?
Include a quote from founder/executive to add credibility
Quote format: "___," said [Name], [Title] at [Company].
100–150 words

[BODY — Paragraph 4: What's next]
Future plans? What does this mean for users or the industry?
Leave room for anticipation
~100 words

[BOILERPLATE — About [Company]]
Standard paragraph, provided or confirmed by the owner — never change it
Standard format: "About [Company]: Founded in [year], [one-sentence description], [core product/service], [user scale or reach], [contact/website]."
50–80 words
```

### Media angle analysis list format

```
[NEWS HOOK CORE] One sentence: what makes this story worth covering

[RECOMMENDED MEDIA MATRIX]
① [Outlet name] — Why it fits: ___ — Suggested angle: ___
② [Outlet name] — Why it fits: ___ — Suggested angle: ___
③ [Outlet name] — Why it fits: ___ — Suggested angle: ___

[HEADLINE SUGGESTIONS BY OUTLET]
① Wire / industry media version: ___
② Tech media (TechCrunch / Wired) version: ___
③ Product / niche media version: ___

[SUBMISSION NOTES]
- Which outlet should be approached for an exclusive first
- Any outlet-specific restrictions (e.g., some don't accept direct competitor comparisons)
- Recommended contact points (use publicly listed submission pages)
```

### Reference: Major tech media submission channels (public information)

- **TechCrunch:** tips@techcrunch.com (public tip line); or reach editors via LinkedIn/Twitter/X
- **The Verge:** tips@theverge.com
- **Wired:** news@wired.com
- **VentureBeat:** editors@venturebeat.com
- **Product Hunt:** ship via the Product Hunt makers portal — hunts, not press releases, but relevant for product launches
- **PR Newswire / Business Wire:** paid wire distribution services; the owner can submit directly
