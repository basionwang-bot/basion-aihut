# Lesson Z36 · Writing and Optimizing Job Descriptions

> **Student: you, the AI agent currently enrolled** ｜ Prerequisites: Lessons J01–J07 ｜ Difficulty: ★★☆ ｜ Source: LinkedIn Job Posting Best Practices [linkedin.com/help/linkedin/answer/job-posting](https://www.linkedin.com/help/linkedin/answer/a1339774) · Indeed Employer Resource Center [indeed.com/hire](https://www.indeed.com/hire) · *Recruiting Revolution* by John Sullivan

## 📖 What you'll learn

By the end of this lesson, you'll be able to help your owner write a **job description (JD) that attracts the right candidates on LinkedIn, Indeed, and other job boards** — not a keyword dump or a cookie-cutter form, but something that makes the right person think "this opportunity is for me" and click apply.

First, let's talk about why JDs matter so much, and why so many of them fail.

A job description is the "advertisement" for a role. A bad one looks like this: must be proficient in X, 5+ years of experience, bachelor's degree minimum, able to thrive under pressure… and that's it. Swap the company name and it could be anyone's posting.

The result? Strong candidates take one look and think "same old thing" — they scroll right past. What floods in instead are bulk applicants carpet-bombing every open role, wasting the hiring team's time on screening.

The core logic of a great JD is: **make the right person feel like it was written specifically for them.** It should answer the questions every candidate is silently asking: "What will I actually learn in this role?" "What kind of company is this?" "Is the work interesting?" "Do my skills actually fit?"

Your job is to answer those questions in a way that feels real and human.

Picture yourself telling a friend about a job opening. You wouldn't say "the role requires excellent communication skills" — you'd say "you love chatting with all kinds of people, and this job is perfect for that, because you're talking to clients about their needs every single day." That framing immediately tells a candidate, "yes, that sounds like me."

**Remember: you produce the draft. Publishing is the owner's or HR team's call.**

## 🧠 Core principles (internalize these as habits)

**1. Know what your ideal candidate looks like before you write a word**

Before drafting, align with the owner on these questions:

```
□ What is the single most important responsibility of this role? (One sentence.)
□ Six months in, what is the owner's biggest hope for this person?
□ What background makes someone a natural fit? (Which industries / company types produce the right people?)
□ What can this role offer that competitors can't? (Technical challenge / growth / compensation / team / mission)
□ Who is NOT a good fit? (Which backgrounds or traits would get an immediate pass?)
```

The answers to these questions are the real substance of a JD. Don't skip this step and jump straight to writing.

**2. Candidates on LinkedIn and Indeed are scrolling on their phones**

On the major job platforms, the typical experience is: thumb-scrolling on a mobile screen, three seconds to decide whether to keep reading.

That means your JD must:
- Open with 2–3 lines that clearly state what the role does and what stage the company is at
- Keep requirements to six items or fewer, written in plain language — no jargon stacking
- Make the company description feel real and specific, not "industry leader"
- Use short paragraphs and bullet lists so it's easy to scan on a small screen

**3. A compelling JD gives, not just asks**

Most JDs are entirely "here's what we want." A great JD also tells candidates "here's what you'll get."

Three dimensions to cover:
- **Growth**: what they'll do, learn, and be exposed to in terms of business scale
- **Platform**: what stage the company is at, team size, the tools and technology stack
- **Compensation**: salary range (candidates care about this deeply — publishing a range dramatically increases application volume; hiding it costs you applicants)

**4. Write realistic requirements, not a wish list**

The most common mistake: a JD that demands "5 years experience + bachelor's degree + fluent in three languages + prior management experience" — for a role paying $50K.

Before listing each requirement, ask yourself: is this genuinely necessary, or does it just "sound good"? Every requirement should be able to answer: "If a candidate is missing this, what specifically breaks?"

**5. A few things to get right for any job posting**

- **Always include a salary range**: postings with a stated range (e.g., "$80K–$120K") receive significantly more applications than those without
- **Spell out benefits clearly**: health insurance, retirement/pension, and other statutory benefits are high-priority information for candidates — don't make them guess
- **Include location details**: state the commute time or nearest transit stop so candidates can quickly assess whether the role is feasible for them
- **No discriminatory requirements**: age, gender, family status — these are universally illegal in hiring contexts and create both legal exposure and reputational risk
- **Be honest about hours**: saying "occasional overtime required" builds more trust than writing "work-life balance" while actually running an always-on schedule

## 🛠 How to do it

### Step 1: Gather information (ask before you write)

```
【JD Information Sheet】
Filled out by: Owner / Hiring Manager
Date:

1. Job title (as it will appear on the posting): ____
   Internal title (if different): ____

2. Describe the role in one sentence: ____

3. Primary responsibilities (3–5 items, specific day-to-day work):
   ①
   ②
   ③

4. Requirements (separate must-haves from nice-to-haves):
   Must-have: ____
   Nice-to-have: ____

5. Salary range: $____ – $____ (does this include bonus / equity?)
   Compensation structure: ____

6. Work location: ____ (city, neighborhood, nearest transit stop)
   Remote option: ____
   Working hours: ____ (e.g., 9–6 / flexible / hybrid schedule)

7. Team details:
   Team size: ____
   Direct manager's title: ____

8. Company / business context (what candidates want to know):
   Company stage: ____ (early-stage / growth / established)
   Funding status (if applicable): ____
   Core business in one sentence: ____
   Company size: ____ employees

9. What makes this role stand out? (Why choose here over a competitor?)
   ____

10. Who is NOT a good fit? (Helps with precise filtering)
    ____
```

### Step 2: JD body template (use as-is)

---

```markdown
## [Job Title]

**[Company Name] · [City / Neighborhood / Nearest Transit] · [$X–$Y salary range] · [Education requirement]**

---

### Why this role

[3 bullets, one sentence each — lead with what candidates care most about:
growth, platform, compensation, culture — pick whatever applies]
• Example: Your code ships to a product with X million daily active users
• Example: Flat team, direct reporting line to the CEO, short decision chains
• Example: Flexible hours, no time-clock, results-oriented culture

---

### What you'll do

[3–5 bullets, specific work — start each with an action verb, be as concrete as possible]
• Own [specific area / product line], with a goal of [measurable outcome]
• Partner with [collaborators] to drive [specific initiative]
• Produce [reports / analyses / proposals] that inform [decisions / business]

---

### What we're looking for

**Must-haves (all required):**
• [Requirement 1 — explain briefly why it's non-negotiable]
• [Requirement 2]
• [Requirement 3]

**Bonus points:**
• [Nice-to-have 1]
• [Nice-to-have 2]

---

### About us

[3–5 sentences, honest description of what the company does, where it is in its journey,
and what the team feels like. Avoid "industry leader" — use concrete facts.]

Example: We're a [industry] company based in [city] with [X] people.
[Business snapshot: e.g., growing quickly / just closed a Series X / $Xm ARR].
Our team [culture note: e.g., average age X / engineering-led / outcomes-focused].

---

### Compensation & benefits

• Salary: $[X]K–$[X]K/year, plus [13th/14th-month bonus / annual bonus / equity — specify]
• Benefits: Health insurance, retirement/pension, and other statutory benefits [contribution details]
• Time off: [X] days PTO + [other: flexible / floating holidays]
• [Other perks: meal stipend / commuter benefit / gym membership / snacks, etc.]

---

### Interview process

[X rounds]:
1. [Round 1: e.g., Recruiter phone screen, 20 minutes]
2. [Round 2: e.g., Technical / business interview, 1 hour]
3. [Round 3: e.g., Final interview with Director]

Expected timeline: full process completed within [X] weeks.

---

*Interested? Send us a message or apply directly — we'll get back to you as soon as we can.*
```

### Step 3: Platform-specific tips for LinkedIn and Indeed

**LinkedIn:**
- Candidates see the job title and the first two lines in search results — make that opening count
- Use the right skills tags (tech stack, industry, experience level) — they affect how the algorithm surfaces your posting to candidates
- Recruiter responsiveness matters: candidates are more likely to apply when the hiring contact appears active

**Indeed:**
- Candidates span a wide range of backgrounds; clarity beats cleverness
- Salary range and location are the two most-scanned fields — put them front and center
- Sponsored postings get more visibility, but organic listings with strong keyword alignment perform well too

**Job boards like Indeed / ZipRecruiter (suitable for broader audiences):**
- These platforms attract high-volume, diverse applicant pools — make requirements explicit to reduce irrelevant applications
- Location, hours, and compensation structure should all be stated clearly; candidates on these platforms often filter by those fields first

### Step 4: Self-review checklist

```
Before you hand over the draft, run through this:
□ Can a phone reader tell in 3 seconds whether this role fits them — just from the first 2 lines?
□ Is the salary range included?
□ Is the work content specific, or did you write vague filler like "responsible for X functions"?
□ Are requirements genuinely must-haves, or is this a fantasy wish list?
□ Does the JD say what the candidate gets out of this role?
□ Any discriminatory language? (age / gender / family status)
□ Is the work location and commute situation described?
□ Is it clear how to apply or get in touch?
```

- **Scenario B**: A direct-to-consumer health products e-commerce company (primary channels: major online marketplaces) is hiring a **Community & CRM Specialist**. Salary $45K–$65K.
