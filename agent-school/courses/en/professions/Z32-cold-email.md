## 📖 What you'll learn

By the end of this lesson, you'll be able to help your owner write a **complete B2B outreach email sequence** — an opening email plus two follow-ups — and produce ready-to-paste, easy-to-customize templates.

First, a quick reality check about cold outreach.

A lot of people think a business email works like this: "Send one email introducing yourself and your product, then wait for a reply." The uncomfortable truth is that this approach gets a response rate of... well under 1%.

Why? Because a cold email is basically a stranger knocking on someone's door without an invitation. Think about it from the recipient's side — you're in the middle of your day, and an email arrives from someone you've never met, raving about how great their company's product is. What's your first instinct?

That's why the core logic of a good outreach email is: **make the reader feel this email was written specifically for them — not blasted to a list of thousands.**

Picture a sharp job applicant. They don't send the same generic resume to every company. Each cover letter says something like: "I saw you just closed your Series B and are expanding into X — I happen to have direct experience there." That's personalization. The more personal the email feels, the higher the reply rate.

Your job is: **draft outreach emails that are highly personalized and follow a coherent sequence logic, tailored to your owner's business and target customers.** Whether to send them, when to send them, and to whom — those decisions belong to your owner and their sales team.

> ⚠️ **Iron rule: you only write drafts — you never send emails. Never connect to your owner's email account. Never trigger any automated sending. Once the drafts are ready, hand them to your owner and let them take it from there.**

## 🧠 Core principles (internalize these as habits)

**1. An email sequence is a rhythm, not a bombardment**

A standard outreach sequence looks like this:

```
Email 1 (Day 1):   Opening — get to the point, explain why you're writing to this specific person
Email 2 (Day 4–5): Follow-up 1 — come at it from a new angle, add value, or ask a specific question
Email 3 (Day 10–12): Follow-up 2 — one final, polite check-in with a clear "end point"
```

After three emails with no reply, stop. Sending more turns you from a salesperson into a nuisance.

**2. The subject line is the front door — if it doesn't open, nothing else matters**

The subject line decides whether the email gets opened at all. Good subject lines share a few traits:
- Specific: not "Regarding a potential partnership" but "A thought on [Company]'s challenge in [industry]"
- Curiosity-inducing: makes the reader feel "this might actually be relevant to me"
- Under 50 characters (so it doesn't get cut off on mobile)

Bad subject lines: `Introduction to our products` / `Exploring a collaboration opportunity` / `[Company] product recommendation`

Good subject lines:
- `An idea for the [Company] team re: [specific topic]`
- `3 ways [industry] companies cut [specific cost] — [Owner's Company]`
- `After speaking with [similar company], thought I'd reach out`

**3. The golden structure for your opening email: AIDA, stripped down**

```
Opening:  Why you're writing to this specific person (personalized hook — show your homework)
Middle:   What problem you can help solve (one sentence, one pain point)
Closing:  A low-friction call to action (not "let's sign a contract" — try "15-minute call?")
```

The whole email should be under 150 words. Shorter is better.

**4. Follow-up emails need a new angle — don't just repeat the opening**

The most common follow-up mistake: "I sent you an email last week — just checking if you saw it?" That adds zero new value.

A good follow-up either shares a relevant case study, asks a thoughtful question, or offers a useful resource (an industry report, a relevant article). Every email needs to give the reader a reason to think "worth opening."

**5. A few B2B outreach habits worth knowing**

- Greeting: Use the person's first name ("Hi Sarah") or their professional title if you know it. Skip generic openers like "Dear Sir/Madam."
- Sign-off: Warm but professional — "Looking forward to your thoughts" or "Happy to connect whenever works for you" lands better than a cold "Regards."
- Timing: Tuesday through Thursday, 9–11 am or 2–4 pm local time tends to see the highest reply rates. Avoid Monday mornings and Friday afternoons.
- LinkedIn follow-up: If three emails go unanswered, consider reaching out via LinkedIn or a relevant industry Slack — but that's a step your owner takes, not you.

## 🛠 How to do it

### Outreach email sequence templates (ready-to-reuse frameworks)

---

**[Opening Email]**

```
Subject: [Personalized hook] + a specific question or value statement

Hi [First Name],

[Personalized opener — 1–2 sentences]: I noticed that [Company] recently [specific
detail, e.g., expanded your sales team / closed a funding round / launched a new
product line] — which made me think…

[Core value — 1 sentence]: We've helped [similar type of company] [specific outcome,
e.g., cut sales follow-up time by 30% / double their lead conversion rate] in
[specific situation].

[Low-friction CTA]: Would you have 15 minutes next week for me to walk you through
how we did it?

[Sign-off]
[Name] | [Title] | [Company]
[Phone] | [Email]
```

---

**[Follow-Up 1 (Day 4–5)]**

```
Subject: Re: [Previous subject] — one data point / case study

Hi [First Name],

I sent you a note last week — hope it didn't land at a bad time.

Wanted to add a concrete example: [Client Company in same industry] was facing
exactly [specific pain point] before they started using [product/service]. Three
months in, [specific, measurable result].

I'm curious whether [Company] runs into similar challenges around [related problem]?

If it makes sense, a 15-minute call could help us figure out whether there's a fit —
or rule it out entirely.

[Sign-off]
```

---

**[Follow-Up 2 (Day 10–12) — Final email]**

```
Subject: Closing the loop on my previous notes

Hi [First Name],

This will be my last proactive reach-out. If the timing isn't right or this isn't a
current priority, I completely understand — no further emails from me.

If things change down the road, feel free to reach out any time: [phone/email].

If you do have even a small amount of interest in [core value, one sentence], just
reply with "let's chat" and I'll handle the scheduling.

Wishing you a great rest of the week.

[Sign-off]
```

### Personalization research checklist (do this before writing the opening email)

Before you write the personalized hook for the opening email, spend 5 minutes on this research (your owner can supply details, or use publicly available information):

```
□ Has the company recently raised funding, expanded, or launched a new product?
  (Check LinkedIn, company news, press releases, Crunchbase)
□ Does their website mention specific challenges, goals, or strategic priorities?
□ Has the contact person posted relevant content on LinkedIn or industry forums?
□ Do you share a mutual connection who could be name-dropped?
  ("We have a mutual contact in [Name]…")
□ Are any similar companies already your owner's clients?
```

Each answer gives you sharper material for the personalized opening line.

### Bulk personalization (for owners working through a list of leads)

If your owner has a leads spreadsheet (from Lesson Z31), you can use Python to generate personalized drafts at scale:

```python
import pandas as pd

# 读取富集后的线索清单
leads = pd.read_excel("enriched_leads.xlsx")

email_template = """Subject: {subject}

Hi {name},

I noticed that {company} recently {hook} — which made me think…

We've helped companies in {industry} tackle {pain_point} and achieve {value}.

Would you have 15 minutes next week for a quick call?

[Sales signature]"""

drafts = []
for _, row in leads.iterrows():
    draft = email_template.format(
        subject=f"A thought for {row['公司名称']} on {row['行业']}",
        name=row["联系人"],
        company=row["公司名称"],
        hook="[请主人填写个性化锚点]",  # 这里必须人工填写
        industry=row["行业"],
        pain_point="[请主人填写痛点]",
        value="[请主人填写价值主张]"
    )
    drafts.append({
        "收件人": row["联系人"],
        "公司": row["公司名称"],
        "邮件地址": row.get("邮箱_清洗", ""),
        "草稿正文": draft
    })

pd.DataFrame(drafts).to_excel("email_drafts.xlsx", index=False)
print(f"已生成 {len(drafts)} 封邮件草稿,请主人填写个性化内容后再发送")
```

> ⚠️ This script only generates an Excel file of drafts — **it does not send any emails.** The personalized hook fields are left blank or marked "[owner to fill in]." Do not let the agent invent details about a prospect's company out of thin air.
