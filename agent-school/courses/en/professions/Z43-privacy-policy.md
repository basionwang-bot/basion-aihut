# Lesson Z43 · Privacy Policy Drafting

> **Student: you, the AI agent currently enrolled** ｜ Prerequisites: Lessons J01–J07, Z42 Compliance Check ｜ Difficulty: ★★☆ ｜ Source: general privacy-law principles; primary frameworks referenced are GDPR (EU, Regulation 2016/679) and CCPA (California Consumer Privacy Act) as widely-recognized models — adapt to the applicable jurisdiction; this is not legal advice, always have the final draft reviewed by a qualified lawyer before publication

---

## 📖 What you'll learn

After this lesson, you will draft a **structurally complete privacy policy** that maps to widely recognized privacy-law principles — not a document that just recites legal boilerplate, but an honest text that real users can read and understand: "what data you collect about me, how you use it, and what rights I have."

**Important disclaimer (required reading — pass this on to the owner):**

> ⚠️ **AI-generated privacy policy drafts are for reference only and do not constitute legal advice.** Privacy policies involve business-specific details, regulatory interpretation, and local enforcement nuances. The draft produced in this lesson is a starting point. **Please have it reviewed by a qualified lawyer before publishing.**

Think of it this way: before surgery, a hospital asks you to sign an informed-consent form — it says "here's what we'll do, here are the risks, here's what you can refuse." A privacy policy is essentially the same kind of informed-consent document between an app and its users. Write it well and users trust you; write it badly or skip it and regulators will come knocking.

---

## 🧠 Core principles (internalize these as habits)

1. **Use a recognized privacy framework as the structural backbone.** Major privacy regimes share a common skeleton: lawful basis for processing, purpose limitation, data minimization, transparency, data security, individual rights, and accountability. Whether the applicable law is GDPR, CCPA, or another national framework, your draft should be able to map each section back to at least one of these principles.

2. **Write in plain language, not legalese.** "Subject to your prior consent, we shall process your personal data in accordance with applicable laws and regulations" — who understands that? Better: "We only start using your personal data after you agree to it, and we'll explain exactly how we use each type of data in the sections below." Privacy policies are written for ordinary people, not for courtrooms.

3. **Enumerate every category of data you actually collect — no catch-all "other information."** Help the owner inventory exactly what data the product collects: phone number? Email? Location? Device identifiers? Payment details? Biometrics? For each category, specify the collection purpose, use, and retention period. A vague "other information" bucket is one of the most common regulatory complaint points worldwide.

4. **The user-rights section must be actionable, not decorative.** Major privacy laws give users rights: access, portability, correction, deletion, withdrawal of consent, objection to processing, and more (the exact set depends on the jurisdiction). Your draft should not just say "you have these rights" — it should tell users *how* to exercise them: which email address to write to, which menu in the app to navigate to, how many days the owner has to respond. Empty promises are useless.

5. **A draft is a draft; publication happens after the lawyer signs off.** What you produce is a starting point for the owner and their legal counsel to revise — not something that can be clicked "live" directly.

## 🛠 How to do it

### Step 1: Gather business information (confirm before writing)

Before writing a single word, confirm these seven things with the owner:

```
□ Product name and legal name of the operating entity?
□ Product type? (App / mini-program / website / SaaS?)
□ Does the user base include minors? (If yes, what age thresholds apply in the target jurisdiction,
  and does parental/guardian consent apply?)
□ What personal data is collected? (Phone number / email / location / device identifiers /
  payment details / biometrics / browsing behavior …)
□ Is personal data shared with third parties? (Ad SDKs / payment processors / analytics tools?)
□ Is data stored locally or transferred internationally? (Cross-border transfer rules vary widely)
□ Contact information for privacy inquiries (complaint email / customer service channel / DPO
  contact if GDPR applies)?
□ Primary jurisdiction(s) where users are located?
```

### Step 2: Standard chapter structure for a privacy policy

A well-structured privacy policy should include the following chapters (adapt based on your applicable law):

```
1. What personal data we collect and why
   1.1 Categories of data we collect
   1.2 Legal basis for processing (GDPR: consent / contract / legitimate interest / legal obligation;
       CCPA: categories and purposes; or applicable local framework)
   1.3 How we use the data
   1.4 Cookies and similar technologies (if applicable)

2. How we share, transfer, and disclose your personal data
   2.1 Third-party sharing (which partners, under what conditions)
   2.2 Business transfers (mergers, acquisitions)
   2.3 Public disclosure

3. How we protect your personal data
   3.1 Technical safeguards
   3.2 Organizational safeguards
   3.3 Data-breach response

4. Your rights
   4.1 Right to access and portability
   4.2 Right to correction
   4.3 Right to deletion ("right to be forgotten")
   4.4 Right to withdraw consent / opt out of sale (CCPA) / object to processing (GDPR)
   4.5 Account deletion
   4.6 How to exercise your rights (specific steps, response timeline)

5. Children's data (if applicable)
   — Applicable age threshold in the target jurisdiction
   — Parental/guardian consent mechanism

6. International data transfers (if data is transferred across borders)
   — Legal mechanism used (e.g., Standard Contractual Clauses, adequacy decision, or equivalent)

7. How this policy is updated
   — How users are notified of changes
   — Effective date

8. How to contact us
   — Privacy contact email
   — Data Protection Officer contact (if required by GDPR or equivalent)
   — Response timeline (GDPR: typically 30 days; verify for your jurisdiction)
```

### Step 3: High-risk clause checklist

After drafting, check these commonly problematic points:

```
□ Bundled / blanket consent — using a single checkbox to cover all purposes at once?
  (Most modern privacy laws require granular, specific consent for different purposes)
□ Sensitive categories (health, biometrics, precise location, financial data, religious / political
  beliefs) — are they identified separately and subject to explicit / heightened consent?
□ Are all third-party SDKs and data processors listed? (Advertising, analytics, payment, maps —
  each must be disclosed)
□ Is data retention period specified for each category? ("The maximum period permitted by law"
  is usually insufficient — give a specific period or criteria for deletion)
□ Are cross-border data transfers disclosed with the legal mechanism identified?
□ Is the privacy policy actively presented to users at registration / first use? (Not just buried
  in a footer)
□ For GDPR: is a lawful basis stated for each processing purpose?
□ For CCPA: is the right to opt out of the sale of personal information described?
□ Is there a DPO appointed and contactable (if required under GDPR or local equivalent)?
```

### Step 4: Output format requirements

- Title: `[Product Name] Privacy Policy`
- At the top: "Effective date: [YYYY-MM-DD]" and "Version: v1.0 (Draft)"
- At the bottom: "This draft was generated with AI assistance for reference only. It does not constitute legal advice. Please have it reviewed by a qualified lawyer before publishing."
