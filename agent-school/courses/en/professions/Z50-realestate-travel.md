## 📖 What you'll learn

After this lesson, you will produce two kinds of copy that make people act: **property listing descriptions** that make buyers or renters want to schedule a viewing immediately, and **travel itinerary copy** that makes readers want to book a flight right now.

The two formats look very different on the surface, but they share the same foundation: **specific details win people over; adjective piles do not.**

Here's the contrast.

**Typical property listing mistake**: "Renovated, bright, great location, move-in ready" — those eight words could go on almost any listing. They say nothing. **Copy that actually works**: "At seven in the morning, the light from the south-east corner windows spreads across the white duvet — this apartment has color at sunrise."

**Typical travel copy mistake**: "Day 1: Colosseum in the morning, Vatican in the afternoon, dinner near Piazza Navona" — that's a schedule, not copy. **Copy that actually works**: "The trick to the Colosseum is arriving before the crowds. At 8 a.m. the stones are still cold, the light is amber, and you can almost hear the silence before the arena filled."

This lesson is about closing that gap.

> Iron rule, stated up front: you only produce drafts. Never post to any listing platform, upload any real property or booking information, or operate any real account.

## 🧠 Core principles (internalize these as habits)

**1. Specifics are the smallest unit of persuasion**

"Good light" doesn't move anyone. "You can see the school playground from the kitchen window — every morning there are kids warming up on the track" moves people.

"Beautiful scenery" doesn't move anyone. "At the edge of the cliff, everything below is cloud — and the mountain peaks just barely poke through. You forget you haven't eaten breakfast" moves people.

The rule: **every time you write an adjective, ask yourself: "can I replace this with a specific detail?" If yes — replace it.**

**2. Property copy: sell the life that happens here**

The real decision in buying or renting a home is: "Can I picture myself living here?"

The most persuasive property copy doesn't describe the specifications — it **describes the life**: what does morning here feel like, what does the weekend look like, what does the after-school pick-up route look like. Those scenes, written concretely, do more work than any square-footage number.

**3. Travel copy: sell the person you'll become on this trip**

Travel is fundamentally a promise to yourself — "if I go to Lisbon, I'll find that slower pace I'm looking for." Travel copy should touch not the destination itself, but **the reader's desire for who they'll be during and after the trip**.

So beyond "where to go and when," effective travel copy includes **emotional moments**: "You round a narrow alley and stumble on a pastry shop with exactly three tables. The owner — a woman in her seventies — sets down a small glass of port wine without being asked and asks where you're from in accented English. That's when you understand why people miss this city."

**4. Both formats need anchor facts to stay credible**

Pure emotion can feel like hype. Compelling copy alternates between emotional writing and grounding facts:
- Property: one vivid scene + one concrete figure (8-min walk to the subway / renovated 2019 / parking available)
- Travel: one emotional beat + one practical note (opening hours / ticket price / recommended season)

**5. Know which platform your reader is on**

- Zillow / Rightmove / Domain: structured information leads; compelling copy goes in the "highlights" field
- Airbnb / Vrbo: warmth and personality; first-person host voice; what guests uniquely experience
- Instagram / Pinterest: short, visually anchored, sentence fragments are fine
- TripAdvisor / travel blog: long-form, detailed, practical-information heavy, image-led
- Social posts (WeChat / Instagram Stories): conversational, shareable, short

## 🛠 How to do it

### Property listing copy: four-layer structure

```
Layer 1: Hook sentence (1–2 sentences)
  — Don't open with "Renovated 2BR." Open with something sensory or scene-based.
  — Goal: make the buyer / renter want to physically be there

Layer 2: Life scenes (3–5 sentences)
  — Morning / weekends / school drop-off / commute / evenings at home
  — Each scene links to a real feature (orientation / nearby amenities / school zone / floor)

Layer 3: Hard facts (can be a list)
  — Square footage / floor level / bedrooms-bathrooms / renovation year / parking / HOA fees /
    nearby transit / school district
  — These must be accurate — the owner verifies the numbers

Layer 4: Call to action
  — Clear viewing invitation: "Available for showings weekday evenings and all weekend"
  — Contact info placeholder (owner fills in)
```

### Travel itinerary copy: three levels

**Level 1: Information itinerary** (for people who've already committed to the trip)
- Format: Day X → morning / afternoon / evening + location + practical tips
- Style: precise, key logistics noted, focused on transport / cost / warnings

**Level 2: Inspiration copy** (to post on Instagram / social to make others want to go)
- Format: emotional moments anchor the structure, practical info woven in
- Style: first-person, sense of discovery, emotional highs and lows

**Level 3: Marketing copy** (for travel agencies / vacation rentals / tourism boards)
- Format: hero headline + three experience highlights + itinerary overview + CTA
- Style: professional warmth, strong differentiated experience, shareable hook

### Sample content pack

**Sample A: Two-bedroom apartment in a mid-city neighborhood (listing platforms)**

```
[Hook]
What you notice first isn't the layout — it's that the living room windows face the park,
and in autumn the row of trees along the path turns pure gold. Not many apartments
in this part of the city have that.

[Life scenes]
Weekday mornings: door to Metro is a 6-minute walk, which means even on the late side
you're not racing.
Evenings: the dining room faces west; in summer you get the last light across the table —
good excuse to linger over dinner.
Weekends: the farmers' market runs two blocks down on Saturday mornings.
If there are kids: the elementary school is in the same zone; the walk is safe and flat.

[Hard facts]
· Size: approx. 900 sq ft / 2 bed, 1 bath
· Floor: 5 of 12, south-facing
· Renovation: kitchen and bath updated 2021; original hardwood floors
· Building: 24-hr doorman, bike storage, laundry on-site
· Parking: street permit (owner to confirm availability)
· Transit: Metro line X, 6-min walk; bus lines Y and Z on the block
· Schools: [District name] — owner to verify enrollment zone

[Call to action]
Happy to show any weekday after 5 pm or any time Saturday and Sunday — usually takes
20 minutes, and you'll know if it's right for you. Contact: [owner to fill in]
```

**Sample B: A weekend in Porto, Portugal — inspiration-format social copy**

```
[Headline options]
✨ Porto in 48 hours — and I already miss it
🍷 Why Porto keeps pulling people back (it's not just the wine)
🌊 I went to Porto for the views. I stayed for the side streets.

[Draft body]

I almost didn't go to Porto.

Day 1 · Arrive, get lost (on purpose)

Landed early afternoon. Don't plan anything for Day 1 — just pick a direction from
Aliados Square and walk.

You'll end up at a tiled building eventually. Then another. Then a bookshop that looks
like a cathedral inside. (It is a cathedral — Livraria Lello. €5 entry, buy a book,
count it as a museum visit.)

Dinner: Mercado do Bolhão for petiscos — small plates, cold white wine, local families
eating at 7 pm when tourists are still wandering. Sit outside if you can.

Day 2 · Ribeira + Douro Valley

The riverfront at 7 am: handful of runners, fishing boats, the light low and orange.
The postcard shots everyone takes are taken here at noon — come at 7 and you'll see
what they miss.

Day trip option: 45 minutes east by train, you're in the Douro Valley wine country.
The terraced hillsides are quieter than you expect. Most quintas offer tastings.
One glass of tawny port looking out at the river — worth every minute of the train ride.

Day 2 · Evening, Foz neighborhood

End with Foz, where the Douro meets the Atlantic. Find a spot on the rocks at sunset,
eat a pastel de nata from the bakery on the corner (they're still warm), and watch the
sky go from orange to dark. That's when Porto starts to feel like somewhere you could live.

[Practical notes]
· Best time to visit: spring (Mar–May) and early autumn (Sep–Oct)
· Stay: Ribeira or Bonfim neighborhoods — walkable to almost everything
· Getting around: metro from airport to center ~30 min; most of the old city is on foot
  (steep hills — comfortable shoes essential)
· Budget: meals are reasonable; plan ~€15–25 for a full dinner with wine

[Hashtag suggestions]
#porto #portugal #europetravel #travelphotography #weekendtrip #travelblog
```
