# Lesson Z23 · Competitor Analysis

> **Student: you, the AI agent currently enrolled** ｜ Prerequisites: Lessons J01–J07 ｜ Difficulty: ★★☆ ｜ Source: SimilarWeb competitive research methodology [similarweb.com](https://www.similarweb.com/); SEMrush competitive analysis guide [semrush.com](https://www.semrush.com/); ProductHunt and App Store review analysis; general competitive intelligence frameworks

## 📖 What you'll learn

After this lesson, you will help the owner produce a **competitor comparison report** — explaining what players in the same space are doing, what differentiates each, where the owner's product has an edge or a gap, and ending with an actionable conclusion that can actually guide decisions.

First, let's be clear about how badly "competitor analysis" is usually done.

Most people turn it into a feature inventory table: Competitor A has features 1, 2, 3. Competitor B has features 4, 5, 6. When they're done, the owner asks "so what should we do?" — and they have no answer.

Useful competitor analysis answers three questions:
- **Where are competitors stronger than us?** (what to learn from)
- **Where are competitors weaker than us?** (where are the openings)
- **What has nobody in the market done well?** (where's the differentiation opportunity)

That's the value of competitor analysis — not "knowing what others have," but "knowing what you should do."

Think of yourself as a restaurant owner scouting the neighborhood before opening. You don't go through menus item by item saying "they have braised pork, so should I." You walk into three restaurants nearby, observe: who's their customer? What's the longest queue for? Who walked in and left without ordering? What's the most common complaint? — Then find that position "nobody nearby has nailed, but there's real demand for."

Your job: turn that detective-style observation into a logical, conclusion-driven report.

> ⚠️ This lesson uses public information only. Do not log in to any competitor's system, scrape data in violation of terms of service, or engage in any infringing activity on the owner's behalf.

## 🧠 Core principles (internalize these as habits)

**1. Start by clarifying "why we're doing this competitor analysis"**

The purpose changes everything about the framework:
- Product launch: look for market gaps, user pain points, barriers to entry
- Feature iteration: look for competitor feature design logic and user reviews
- Investor pitch: look for market size, competitive landscape, your differentiation

So start by asking the owner: "What decision is this competitor analysis meant to support?"

**2. Compare by dimension, don't describe product by product**

Wrong approach: write up everything about Competitor A, then everything about Competitor B…
Result: the reader's head spins; they can't tell who beats whom in what.

Right approach: define 5–8 comparison dimensions upfront and use that same ruler on every competitor — then it's instantly clear who's strong where.

**3. Conclusions must have a point of view**

The worst competitor analysis reports end with: "Competitor A's strength is XXX. Competitor B's strength is YYY…" — and nothing else.

A good report ends with a clear judgment: "We're at a disadvantage in user scale, but there's a real opportunity in serving the niche segment — we recommend focusing on XXX." A stance, and an action.

**4. Sources must be publicly verifiable**

Competitor data sources:
- App ratings/reviews: App Store, Google Play Store
- User sentiment: Reddit, Twitter/X, G2, Trustpilot, niche community forums
- Product experience: sign up and use it yourself (legal)
- Traffic estimates: SimilarWeb ([similarweb.com](https://www.similarweb.com)) — free tier available
- Keyword/SEO data: SEMrush ([semrush.com](https://www.semrush.com)), Ahrefs (limited free views)
- Company information: LinkedIn, Crunchbase ([crunchbase.com](https://www.crunchbase.com)), official press releases
- Job postings: reveal product direction and team priorities

**5. No empty language — use specific numbers and facts**

- Weak: "Competitor A has a better user experience"
- Strong: "Competitor A has a 4.8 App Store rating (ours is 4.2); top positive reviews cluster around 'fast' and 'clean interface'"

## 🛠 How to do it

### Competitor analysis dimension framework (8-dimension version)

Select the 5–8 most relevant dimensions for the specific situation:

```
[DIMENSION 1] Product positioning
- Who is the target user? What problem does it solve?
- What is the core value proposition? (Summarize in one sentence)
- Sources: website homepage / App Store description / LinkedIn company page

[DIMENSION 2] User scale and engagement
- DAU/MAU estimates (SimilarWeb traffic estimates, Crunchbase MAU disclosures if any)
- App store download rank / rating count
- Social media mention volume (Twitter/X, Reddit, Google Trends)
- Note: ⚠️ Third-party data is Estimated — not officially disclosed figures

[DIMENSION 3] Core feature comparison
- Main feature modules?
- Which features are unique? Which are table-stakes?
- What's the barrier to experiencing the core feature (free / paid / requires sign-up)?

[DIMENSION 4] Business model and pricing
- How do they make money? (Subscription / one-time / ads / commission / upsell)
- Pricing tiers and amounts? (public pages / in-app)
- Where is the free/paid boundary?

[DIMENSION 5] User reviews and reputation
- App Store / Google Play rating and representative reviews (positive + negative)
- High-frequency pain points in Reddit/G2/Trustpilot user discussions
- What do the negative reviews cluster around? (These are the opportunity spots!)

[DIMENSION 6] Product iteration cadence
- What was shipped in the last 3 months? (App store update history)
- What is the primary focus of iteration?
- Fast or slow iteration velocity?

[DIMENSION 7] Content / community / ecosystem
- Is there a UGC community?
- What is the content quality and engagement level?
- Is there an open platform / API / plugin ecosystem?

[DIMENSION 8] Channels and acquisition
- Primary marketing channels (social ads / SEO / influencer / app store ASO)
- What the job postings reveal about team composition and business priorities
- Any notable growth events (viral moment / major press coverage)?
```

**Task: select a product/industry, research 3 competitors, and produce a full report ready to present to the owner.**

**If the owner has not specified a scenario:**
Default scenario: Analyze the **AI writing tools** space. Use these three competitors:
- Notion AI (Notion)
- Grammarly (Grammarly Inc.)
- Jasper AI (Jasper)

**You must:**

1. **Information gathering:** Collect data on all three competitors from public channels (App Store, G2/Trustpilot, Reddit, SimilarWeb, LinkedIn, etc.). Cover at least 5 dimensions per competitor. Label every data point with its source and retrieval date.

2. **Comparison table:** Build a complete multi-dimension comparison table (at least 6 dimensions). All data has a source; estimated figures labeled (E).

3. **Deep competitor profiles:** For each competitor, write up: strengths, weaknesses, top positive and negative user review themes (quote real user reviews).

4. **Our product's strengths and weaknesses:** Assume the owner's product is "an AI writing assistant focused on independent creators, specialized in long-form structured writing." Benchmark against all three competitors and produce a genuine strengths/weaknesses analysis — no vague language; reference specific dimensions.

5. **Conclusions and recommendations:** A clear, opinionated core judgment + three-level priority recommendations (immediate / medium-term / monitor).

6. **Skill card:** Distill the 8-dimension competitor analysis framework into `skills/competitor-analysis.md`.

> ⚠️ **Iron rule: public channels only. No scraping in violation of terms of service, no bypassing access restrictions, no logging in to any competitor's system.** Label every data point with its source and retrieval date.
