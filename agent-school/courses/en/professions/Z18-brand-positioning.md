## 📖 What you'll learn

After this lesson, you will deliver a **brand positioning document + voice copy pack** — not "our brand values are innovation, quality, and customer-centricity" platitudes, but a one-sentence positioning statement sharp enough to put on a storefront sign, a brand voice guide, and real copy examples for each channel — all four packed together.

Think of yourself as a **brand identity designer**. The restaurant makes great food, but its Yelp page is named "John's Place" with a bio that says "Welcome, quality guaranteed" — no one walking past can tell it apart from the shop next door. Your job is to help the owner **find one sentence that says "who we are and why we're different,"** then weave that sentence into every touchpoint's copy.

**You produce the positioning doc and copy drafts. Final decisions and publishing belong to the owner.**

## 🧠 Core principles

### 1. Positioning means "claiming a slot in the user's mind"

*Positioning*'s central insight: the human brain can hold only 2–3 brands per category. Coca-Cola owns "fun soda." Nike owns "athletic achievement." Stripe owns "developer-first payments." These brands have claimed their mental slot.

Positioning work answers one question: **In my category, which slot do I want to own in the user's mind?** Is that slot empty? If not, can I articulate it more crisply than whoever holds it now?

### 2. A one-sentence positioning must pass three tests

**Test 1 · Fill-in-the-blank test**
> "When a user wants ______ (specific need), they should think of you, because you are ______ (unique value)."

**Test 2 · Contrast test**
> "Unlike ______ (competitor), we are ______ (differentiator)."

**Test 3 · Believability test**
> Will users actually believe this? Is there evidence behind it? (Self-praise with no proof is dismissed.)

All three must pass before the positioning is valid.

### 3. Voice matters more than content — and it's harder to measure

Two brands say the same thing:

Brand A: "Our product is crafted from premium ingredients, subject to rigorous quality control…"
Brand B: "Only the best goes in. For you."

Same message, totally different experience. A is talking to itself; B is talking to the user.

Brand Voice is "if this brand were a person, how do they speak" — a rigorous, professorial expert? A dry, witty neighbor? A warm, quiet confidant? Once the voice is set, every single piece of copy must hold that persona without drifting.

### 4. Positioning and voice are the core; content is the shell

Positioning and voice can hold for years; content refreshes daily. The mark of a truly consistent brand: scroll through three years of their social feed and every post sounds like the same person wrote it.

## 🛠 How to do it

### Standard structure of a brand positioning document

```
Brand Positioning Document (owner's internal reference + external copy foundation):
├── 1-positioning-one-pager.md    Core positioning sentence + three test results + supporting rationale
├── 2-brand-voice-guide.md        Voice vocabulary + do/don't examples across communication scenarios
├── 3-channel-copy-pack.md        Bio/About copy for key platforms + slogan options ×3
└── 4-brand-story.md              ≤200-word "About Us" paragraph for website/profile pages
```

### Positioning workflow (four steps)

**Step 1: Understand "who we are"**
```
Answer these five questions (owner fills in, you organize):
① What do we sell? (Category — be as specific as possible)
② Who are our main customers? (Be concrete — "urban women 25–35 with kids" beats "young women")
③ What is their biggest pain point or desire in this category?
④ Who are our main competitors? What are they communicating?
⑤ What can we do (or say) that they can't — or won't?
```

**Step 2: Distill the one-sentence positioning**

Formula: `[Target audience] + [context/need] + [differentiated value]`

Examples:
- "For parents of babies with sensitive skin — the first moisturizer that shows you every ingredient added, and every ingredient left out."
- "Between ordering delivery and cooking from scratch, there's a third option: a real meal in 15 minutes."

**Step 3: Write the voice guide**

```
Brand persona: If this brand were a person, who are they?
(Example: A 38-year-old mom who spent a decade in product R&D at a consumer goods company.
Speaks directly, not emotionally manipulative, genuinely cares about your kid,
will break down the ingredient list for you, will not upsell you something useless.)

Voice keywords (3–5):
  - Expert but not cold
  - Honest — no exaggeration
  - Caring, not preachy
  - Warm without sentimentality

Do (correct examples):
  ✅ "No fragrance in this moisturizer — not because fragrance is expensive, but because baby skin doesn't need it."
  ✅ "Our test results weren't as perfect as we'd hoped — but they're real."

Don't (forbidden examples):
  ❌ "We're the most professional parent brand!" (vague, self-congratulatory)
  ❌ "All mothers share one heart…" (manipulative, hollow)
  ❌ Every piece of copy mentions the product name in the first line (doesn't sound like a real person)
```

**Step 4: Translate positioning into channel copy**

One positioning → translated into specific words for each channel:

| Channel | Character limit | Copy task |
|---------|----------------|-----------|
| Instagram bio | 150 chars | Make a first-time visitor understand what you do |
| LinkedIn page description | 300 words | Build credibility with a professional audience |
| Website homepage headline | ≤20 words | Grab the visitor in one line |
| Slogan (primary tagline) | ≤10 words | Memorable, spreadable |
| About Us (brand story) | 200 words | Make people feel this brand has warmth and history |

### Real example: Children's skincare brand "PureLittle" (hypothetical)

**I. Positioning one-pager**

Target audience: Urban parents (25–38), children 0–3 years old, have basic ingredient literacy (can read a label), don't trust advertising uncritically.

Core need: Products for their baby must be clean (minimal, purposeful ingredients), effective (barrier support), and pleasant to use (no tight feel, no greasy residue).

Main competitor messaging:
- Japanese imports: heritage, "gentle by Japanese standard"
- Mass-market domestic brands: mom word-of-mouth, affordable
- Niche imports: specialist formulations for specific skin types

Our differentiator: Deep ingredient transparency (published rationale for every ingredient added and omitted) + clinical dermatology partnership for validation

**One-sentence positioning:**
> "The first moisturizer for your baby's skin where we explain every ingredient we chose to add — and every ingredient we chose to leave out."

Fill-in-the-blank: When a parent wants a clean, trustworthy moisturizer for their baby, they should think of PureLittle, because it's the only brand that publicly documents both why each ingredient is included and why others are not.

Contrast test: Unlike other baby brands that just say "gentle," we explain *how* gentle is achieved.

Believability test: Dermatologist partnership + downloadable ingredient rationale sheet per product → credible.

---

**II. Brand voice guide**

Brand persona: A young mother with a chemistry degree who now makes children's skincare. Knows her formulations deeply, doesn't chase trending ingredients, speaks directly without evasion, genuinely cares about the user but doesn't tell them what to think — treats parents as intelligent adults.

Voice keywords: **Transparent / Rational / Warm / No-fluff**

| Voice dimension | Do | Don't |
|----------------|-----|-------|
| Talking about ingredients | "Added oat beta-glucan because it supports skin barrier repair — not because it's trendy, because it works" | "Selected premium botanical extracts for extra nourishment" |
| Talking about results | "In our test, 80% of babies showed reduced redness — not 100%, because every baby's skin is different" | "Works instantly! Visible improvement!" |
| Talking about price | "This version costs $8 more because of an extra processing step — your call" | "Incredible value! So affordable!" |
| Talking to parents | "You can ask me directly why we added this. I'll explain." | "All mothers, rest assured — we do it for the babies!" |

---

**III. Channel copy pack**

**Instagram bio (150 chars)**
> Children's skincare ingredient lab. Every product comes with a full ingredient rationale: why each one is in, and why the others aren't. You read, you decide. Questions? Ask in comments.

**LinkedIn page description (excerpt)**
> PureLittle was built on one question: why are baby skincare ingredient lists getting longer? We believe parents deserve to know not just what's in their child's products, but why. Every formulation ships with a downloadable ingredient rationale. No trade secrets — just transparency.

**Website homepage headline (18 words)**
> Every ingredient in your baby's skincare: we have a reason for each one.

**Slogan options ×3 (≤10 words)**
> A. Less in it. Better for them.
> B. Every ingredient earns its place.
> C. Clean is the best formula.

**Brand story (180 words)**
> PureLittle started with a question: why are baby skincare ingredient lists getting longer?
>
> I'm a mother of two and a trained chemist. When I looked at the baby products on the market, I found something strange: many "gentle" products were full of things a baby's skin simply doesn't need — fragrance, excess preservatives, trend-chasing actives. Added because consumers like them. Not because infant skin requires them.
>
> So in 2021 we built PureLittle: every product uses only what a baby's skin actually needs. Every ingredient has a documented reason for being there. Every ingredient we left out has a reason for being absent. Our ingredient rationale sheets are free to download — we have nothing to hide.
>
> Because if you're going to put something on the most sensitive skin in your home, you deserve to know exactly why.
